Informed / Informal

Notes on the week to 24th April

Of course you want to know what the 14th century English mystic Julian of Norwich has to say about the world right now. Her advice is this: Await. Allow. Accept. Attend. Works for me in so many urgencies. So let's go.

Await!

On Tuesday we'll be exactly two months into the Iran war, which is a long-enough period of awaiting to draw some conclusions. First, all expectations have been confounded. Maybe there were expectations that making the rubble bounce in Tehran would lead to regime collapse. Didn't happen. Maybe there were expectations that devolved and distributed IRGC leadership and armoury would quickly exhaust US interceptors. Didn't happen. Maybe Iran taking hostage the Straits of Hormuz would be an unanswerable weapon against US aggression. Didn't happen. Maybe the Iran war would somehow metastasize into an all-out Middle Eastern war of each against each (and Israel). Didn't happen. Maybe it would rally Arab states around the American flag as never before. Isn't happening. Maybe the closing of the Straits of Hormuz would generate 'the most severe oil shock ever' (IEA). Hasn't happened yet, with Brent still about 16% lower than it was in the immediate aftermath of Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Maybe Iran would sue for peace. No obvious sign of that yet. Maybe US would sue for peace out of political exhaustion, or Trumpian impatience. No obvious sign of that yet.

Awaiting, then, has tumbled almost all mainstream hopes and fears about the cause, conduct, and outcome of the war.

Allow!

It follows that we must allow ourselves to think afresh. In particular, we must allow ourselves to challenge the underlying premises, which tend to be about the character of President Trump, and the US aims in the war on Iran.

I have long been deeply sceptical of the argument that this war is simply the produce of President Trump's stupidity and cupidity, that it was simply a mistake or disaster made of the President's own character flaws. One reason is that as early as 2nd March, people with professional insight into US security circles published a long, detailed and minutely informed explanation about just how this war would 'go wrong'. From 'Epic Fury' to 'Epic Folly', they foresaw that decapitating the Iranian leadership wouldn't topple the regime; they foresaw that the IRGC had in place a 'dead hand' mechanism in which distributed leadership and armoury would maintain a drone threat long into the future; they foresaw the blockade of the Straits of Hormuz. And more: all their 'predictions' have proved accurate.

Most crucially, they also provided an explanation, a ruthless explanation, for US actions which really had nothing to do with regime change, with nuclear potential, or with the democratic aspirations of the Iranian people. More on that later.

The second reason for my rejecting the 'Trump is just a bad, mad man' explanation, is the track record of US strategic thought. Put briefly, the post-WW2 American Empire, despite being generally a benevolent hegemony, has been utterly ruthless in maintaining and advancing its empire. Thus the ruthlessness in which the US dismantled the British Empire, and specifically Sterling's international role at express pace, using the 1948 Anglo-American Financial Agreement. Thus the Rand Corporation's 1950s use of game theory to calculate how the US could wage and win a global nuclear war. Thus the 'Plaza Accords' of the mid-1980s which revalued the yen hard enough to destroy the entangled finances which powered Japan as a credible threat to the US industrial economy. Thus the defence hardball tactics deployed via a genuinely peacenik President Reagan which formed a crucial part of the background to the collapse of the USSR.

It is quite inconceivable that US strategists didn't know about all the pitfalls awaiting them in the Iran War. But they did it anyway. We must allow ourselves to know this: what is happening now is not a 'mistake', it is allowed for in the plan. It hardly matters whether President Trump knows this or not.

Accept!

What, then, might 'the plan' be, accepting that the planned-for result will be to leave the American Empire ever more powerful and entrenched for the foreseeable future. (Perhaps we might also acknowledge that these explanations are likely to be more alarming/disconcerting than the simple Trump's a madman explanation).

I think there are three hints.

First, immediately prior to launching the war on Iran, the US military effectively seized control of Venezuela's oilfields. This alone matters hugely, because Venezuela holds the world's largest proven oil reserves: 303 billion barrels, about 17% of the global total and more than Saudi Arabia's 267 billion. What's more, Venezuela's oil is of a particularly rare 'long-carbon' composition. Those 'long carbons' are needed to make, for example, the asphalt which forms the bedrock of so much infrastructure development (are you listening China?) But there's more: its neighbours Guyana and Suriname also sit on top of an offshore 'super basin' which is in the early stages of being opened, and which may also turn out to have larger reserves than Saudi Arabia.

Second, the response to Iran blocking the Straits of Hormuz has been for the US to block it even more firmly in response. Message: we (the US) really don't need that oil — we've got plenty. The 'most severe oil shock ever' is for other countries to worry about, not the US.

Third, at the IMF/World Bank Spring Meetings, the UAE (foreign reserves c$280bn) filed a request for a dollar/dirham swap facility with the Federal Reserve Bank. This is probably best viewed not as an immediate need, but rather as a signal that perhaps when China becomes the dominant buyer of their petrochemicals, they'll be selling in Renminbi rather than dollars. A fundamental threat, in other words, to the role of the dollar in global energy markets, coupled with a direct financial request about how to make it go away.

With the Straits of Hormuz closed, substantial portions of the world's tanker fleets have changed direction and are heading straight to the US refineries clustered on the Gulf Coast. The global market concentration of refineries, post Venezuela, post-Straits of Hormuz, has never been higher. The chaos sown in the Middle East above all generates incomparable strategic leverage for the US, particularly against potential rival powers in China and, possibly, Europe. (In particular: where else can China get its asphalt?) It is also a challenge to Canada's pricing, since most of its oil production gets refined in the US, where spare capacity is likely to be difficult to find in the short/medium/long term.

As for the 'end of the world', or 'World War III' or 'worst oil shock ever', we might consider Pascal's Wager as a reason to be sceptical. After the initial shock, what the current economic data tells us is that economies, and particularly industrial economies, are reacting with practiced vigour to the threat. Most companies have, after all, experienced a lasting energy shock after Russia invaded Ukraine — and, as we have noticed, that had a greater immediate impact on oil prices than the Iran war.

This week's April PMIs were unusually interesting in that respect. They reported that the world is scrambling hard to secure supply, early and at full price, just as consumer confidence is collapsing, and with it, likely short to medium-term demand (seen in the services PMIs).

Japan: manufacturing PMI jumped 3.3pts to 54.9, the highest since Feb 2014, whilst services PMI (more dependent on domestic demand) fell 2.2pts to 51.2, the weakest since May 2025. In Europe, France's manufacturing PMI rose 2.8pts to 52.8, the best since May 2022; UK's manufacturing PMI rose 2.6pts to 53.6, also the best since May 2022. By contrast, Germany's services PMI fell 4pts to 46.9, the weakest since Nov 2022, and France's fell 2.3pts to 46.5, the worst since Feb 2025.

In Europe a whole slew of confidence indicators also fell out of the sky: Zew survey expectations for Eurozone and Germany both slumped to the worst since 2022; Germany's Ifo expectation was the worst since 2023; Eurozone consumer confidence was the worst since 2022, France's the worst since 2023.

There will be more to come from Asia soon: S Korea's consumer confidence fell 7.3%, the prospective conditions down 11.2%.

This pattern strongly suggests that we're unlikely to see a repeat of the 2021–2023 inflation: rather supply is ratcheting up at historically high rates just as consumer confidence is slumping at historically high rates. The result is not sustained inflation, nor sustained high inflation, rather it is a body blow to growth.

Attend!

When I attend to this, I reach the conclusion that what we are seeing is not a 'mistake' nor even a global catastrophe, but rather a re-imagining of the world's energy markets which puts the US, and in particular, its refining capacity, in a controlling position it has not enjoyed or exploited since before WW2.

If so, the distribution of economic and financial activity is being fundamentally disturbed, whose general tendency is a shift towards the US, and the replacement of the 'petro-dollar' strategic imperatives with a simpler US hegemony. What this means for Asia and Europe remains unclear, with the repercussions likely to be non-linear (ie, chaotic to predict), but generally stressful for those regions.

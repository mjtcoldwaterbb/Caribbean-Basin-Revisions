A coherent account of the Caribbean Basin is rarely attempted, because the enormous diversity of history, culture, politics, economics, finance and security make it a challenge indeed. In terms of history alone, the legacies of domination by Spain, Portugal, the Netherlands, France, Britain since the 16th century are all rather different. What they all share, of course, is that they all brought slavery and slaves to the Caribbean, and their ancestors largely populate the Basin. Most, but not all, also were theatres of war between these European nations. Most, but not all, are also infrequently devastated by hurricanes.

These diverse inheritances mean the nations of the Basin rarely genuinely see eye-to-eye or share the same assumptions about the issues and choices which they face. Time and again, when pressed, these differences flare up. In the aftermath of World War 2 Britain attempted to forge together a West Indian Federation (WIF) of all Britain's contemporary West Indian interests, principally Jamaica, Trinidad and Barbados, and scatter of small islands. The WIF lasted just over four years before collapsing in the face of competing and divergent interests (and distrust of British intentions). Fundamentally, the national interests of Jamaica in the north of the Basin were quite distinct from those of Trinidad 1,200 miles to the south. The WIF ghost lives on only in the University of the West Indies (UWI) and the West Indian cricket team. Caricom, its successor, has the sort of mixed record one might expect: high on intentions and declarations, but with only limited progress in melding together the region into a coherent economic or political mass.

So right at the outset, the task of describing and tracking the fundamental dynamics of the Caribbean Basin in the hope of divining its possible future trajectories seems foolhardy. The more so that although I have lived in Barbados for five years, I have no obvious cultural affinity to the Caribbean (I'm from Yorkshire in the UK - and you don't get much less Caribbean than that.). That's why Caribbean Revisions is exactly that - a Living Essay attempting a description, and then recognizing and correcting error. It must evolve continuously, as the realities governing the Basin themselves change. It is not only my ignorance that will be challenged: so will the fundamentals of the essay, as the fundamentals of the Basin themselves change.

The guiding methodology of this Living Essay I take from the 14th century English mystic and anchorite, St Julian of Norwich. Her advice, or technique, is this: Await, Allow, Accept, and Attend. The 4As. For the Revisions, it means I Await by allowing all possible information to wash over me. I Allow that information to challenge everything I know, or add to the fund of my knowledge. I Accept this will uncover my shortcomings, my errors, my stupidities. And finally, I Attend to the consequences. The essay is Revised. And we never stop Awaiting, Allowing, Accepting and Attending.

The Opportunity Why do this? After its extraordinary importance in the 17th to early-19th century, the Caribbean has essentially disappeared from history, becoming merely a picaresque backwater beloved of tourists. But its earlier importance reflected something real: its position offshore major economies as those economies began to expand. What we see in the 21st century is a retreat in global importance of European economies, and the focus of the world's opportunities resting on Asia and the US, and specifically the US South. This is mainly, of course, the rise of the AI / Space economies, but in Texas and Florida, with Caribbean islands only a hop away. But in addition, there is the sudden rise in importance of the US oil industry, and its infant offshoots in Central America (Venezuela, Guyana, possibly still Trinidad).

This is new information, and it is clearly important to the overall conclusions to take from the Essay

The most recent development (first week of June) is that Barbados is inviting bids to develop an oil & gas field found approximately 100km off its coastline. In the north of the basin, of course, the possibility of radical change in Cuba would similarly re-draw the map of the region, and usher it into new international 'interest'.

Suddenly, the Caribbean finds itself in the right place strategically for the first time in over a hundred years. How Caribbean nations deal with the possibilities these dynamics raise will, I think, be an enduring focus of the Revisions. One conclusion which is inescapable from this essay is that the likely winner from the development of local oil & gas fields is Barbados, uniquely suited as it is to hosting management and service-related company regional HQs. It is likely to be transformational.

The Caribbean Basin may be re-entering the world's consciousness, rather in the way that 40-50 years ago, Southeast Asia became 'interesting' for the first time since the 18th century. So there are now dynamics at work in North and Central America which are likely to raise the stakes for the rest of the Caribbean Basin.

What matters is the degree to which the Caribbean nations are in a position to grasp at opportunities which may be coming their way. Which nations have what it takes to transcend the relative torpor in which they have languished for so long? That is what I will explore, and ultimately to collate a "League of Opportunity" ranking these nations. In an attempt to keep things manageable, I'm going to concentrate on Caricom's Big Three but in addition to Guyana, I'm also going to include two of the larger countries in the north of the Basin: Cuba and the Dominican Republic.

But a warning: on a bike ride, even if the net gradient is flat, most of the time it feels like uphill work, because uphill takes time, but downhill is over in a flash. Dealing with Caribbean potential is a bit like that - the hindrances are obvious whilst the potential is not. For much of the time, also, we will be negotiating the 'Imperial Debris' which litters the Caribbean. That debris is everywhere, showing up in kinks in the legal system, trade patterns, migration and re-migration routes, linguistic heritage, plantation geographies and any number and type of inherited vulnerabilities. Countries which can most successfully pick their way through or over that debris-field are likely to have an easier time of adapting to the possibilities which are now opening up. Not that there is a single 'correct' way through the maze - different empires left different debris.

First, let's get the basic right: demographics and current general economic condition.

Demographics

If we take just the 15 members of Caricom, the demographic problem leaps out. It is a vast area, covering roughly 4.1mn to 5.1mn km2 of the globe, but that's almost all ocean. From north to south, it stretches from the Bahamas down to Guyana and Suriname. From West to East, it covers from eastern coast of Central America to Barbados in the Western Atlantic. In that ocean, the amount of land belonging to Caricom members is only 458,480km2.

Scattered islands, in other words, and small ones at that. Caricom's population came to only 19.2mn in 2023, and of those, 11.7mn live in the social/political/financial meltdown that is Haiti. Of the functioning Caricom members, the big three are Jamaica 2.8mn, Trinidad and Tobago 1.4mn. Guyana c780k, and Barbados - which in almost all ways punches above its demographic weight - only around 270k. Exclude the 'big boys', and the mean average population of Caricom nations is just 259k, and the median only 149k.

So the problem Caricom struggles with is very obvious: these islands need an organization which can aggregate their interests and represent them to the rest of the world. But those interests do not necessarily align, and, with such small populations, all politics is always primarily local. For most of these nations, you can collapse the 'six degrees of separation' idea down to two degrees.

At the national level, such small populations face crucial questions of demographic survival. In general terms, a population is thought to be stable if there is a total fertility rate (TFR) of 2.1: ie, that each woman aged 15-49 bears 2.1 children. The Caribbean as a whole fell below that sustainable TFR of 2.1 in 2012, and by 2024 had fallen to 1.68. For Jamaica the TFR is 1.35, for Trinidad & Tobago it is 1.53, for Barbados 1.71, but for Guyana it is still 2.39. Well done Guyana!

The problems generated by this decline in fertility are obvious and shared: an aging population with rising dependency ratios, a drag on overall growth which restricts opportunities for young Caribbeans, and which consequently encourages a negative feedback loop of emigration.

It also raises the economic and cultural importance of the Caribbean diaspora - another field of colonial debris to be negotiated, and one which can be tricky for those on both sides of the field.

The region's demographics are a genuine mixture of the good, the bad and the ugly. Guyana and the Dominican Republic are looking good. Guyana's population is currently rising 0.6% a year, and with a TFR of 2.3, median age of 26.5yrs, rising life expectancy and falling infant mortality, it will probably continue to grow. Similarly, the Dominican Republic's 11.6mn population is growing 0.8% a year, with a median age of 28.7yrs, thanks to a TFR of 2.2.

The 'bad' is actually 'not so bad' yet. Trinidad & Tobago's population is still growing, by around 0.14% a year, but its median age in 2026 was 38.2, up from 26.4 back in 2000. It lost a sustainable TFR back in the early 1990s, but has been roughly stable at 1.5-1.6 for the last 20yrs. Small rises in life expectancy and infant death ratios have kept its population almost stable. Similarly, Barbados's small population is essentially stable despite a TFR which plunged to around 1.7 in 1980 and has stayed there. Gently rising life expectancy (Barbados has the 2nd-highest proportion of centenarians after Japan, apparently) and has pushed the average age now to 39.7yrs, up from 32.4 in 2000.

But for Jamaica and Cuba the demographics are genuinely ugly. Jamaica's population is currently shrinking about 0.13% a year, and the median age has risen to 33.3 years, up from 22.3 in 2020. It last achieved the sustainable population TFR of 2.1 back in 2005. Cuba has a population of 10.9mn, but it's shrinking 0.4% a year, with a median age of 42.5, and a TFR or only 1.5. It hasn't seen a TFR above 2 since 1980. In short, Cuba has a dreadful demographic problem (Cubans don't breed in captivity).

General Economic Situation

Below is a snapshot of the underlying economic diversity recorded by the World Bank for 2024 and 2025. What leaps out immediately is the divergence in accomplishment: Barbados and now Guyana have per capita GDPs around $25-30k, whilst Jamaica, Cuba and even the Dominican Republic struggle to achieve $10k. There are also huge differences in current account positions, with Barbados in deficit to the tune of 7.5% of GDP, whilst Guyana's new oil wealth gives it a surplus of 13.9%. Most of these economies are growing, mostly modestly, but Cuba and Jamaica shrank in 2024. The most curious feature is surely Guyana's unemployment rate of 12%, a result which suggests that the new fountain of oil-wealth has yet to bring wide prosperity to a population which, perhaps, is so far ill-equipped to service the new industries arising.

Infrastructure and Administrative Capacity

Guyana's mixture of extraordinary economic growth with extensive unemployment points to something absolutely crucial: whether a country can fully exploit the advantages which can be won as the region backs into a more visible position in the world. In turn that will depend on the extent to which it can show or develop flexible administrative competence and command the resources to grow useful infrastructures. The factors in place which help to determine that are governmental, human, and circumstantial/historic.

Larger government is not necessarily a guarantee of improved administrative capacity: a large but noticeably incompetent administration is as much a curse as a blessing. It can all too easily become parasitic upon its population, restricting the useful freedoms needed to grasp at opportunities. By contrast, however, too small a government will simply not have the capacity to organize infrastructures, enhance public goods (such as education) or enforce an orderly environment by combating pernicious crime.

Freedom and Crime

Perhaps the most important measure of administrative capacity is a government's ability to maintain law and order. Similarly, a country which is unable to administer a generally safe and predictable environment is unlikely to win the trust of either domestic or international investors. Without that trust, opportunities which arise are likely to be missed. And there is no hiding away from the fact that in many Caribbean countries - most, in fact - crime is a serious problem, a serious impediment to progress.

The World Population Review compiles an annual index of how much crime is committed in a country compiled by looking at the number of reported crimes per head of population. A low score on this count is a good score, a high score bad. Similarly, it is no prize to be towards the top of this list (Trinidad & Tobago #7, Jamaica #9, Guyana #13). The reverse of the crime index score is the safety index score. Generally, you would hope for a safety index/crime index score of >1. This is achieved only by Cuba and Barbados.

Once again, the stand-out exception is Barbados, which comes way down the list (scoring better than both the US and UK), thanks to low scores for criminal markets and criminal actors, and a significantly high score for resilience .

The Resilience Score marks the effectiveness of a country’s judicial system, law enforcement, and victim and witness support. Apart from Barbados, the country with the biggest surprise on this account is Jamaica. Despite Jamaica's dreadful crime index position, it scores the second highest on the 2025 resilience score. This is almost certainly testament to the dramatic efforts made by the Jamaican government since 2024-2025 to toughen sentencing laws, beef up the island's domestic security forces, and an aggressive push against gang-violence and gun crime. As a result, we can expect that Jamaica's crime index score will fall, possibly sharply, in the next review.

Although the results for general criminality are bad news for the Basin, there is better news when it comes to the Global Organized Crime Index: whilst Colombia and Mexico rank highly in this list, thanks to the number of Criminal Actors (Colombia) and Criminal Markets (Mexico), generally the Caribbean isn't a hub of organized crime. Transparency International also published annually a Corruption Perception Index, ranking countries from zero (worst corruption) to 100 (least corruption). As with most other readings, Barbados stands out as a perceived cleaner administration than we see elsewhere in the region. But before you look at the standings of others in this table, consider this: even the Dominican Republic, which ranks a poor #99 in these world rankings, still fares better than, for example, Argentina, Brazil, Indonesia, Thailand, the Philippines, Turkey, Mexico. In none of those countries has the fear of corruption acted as a fatal drag on foreign investment.

Meanwhile Barbados is considered a less corrupt country than either the US or France, Spain, Italy!

And finally, Freedom!

Administrative capacity, including the size of the state, and including public safety, can be bought at the expense of freedom. Conversely, insufficient state capacity can result in a level of freedom which becomes a 'free-for-all' in which security is not guaranteed. Below are the results of the global Freedom Index published by Freedom House, and the Human Freedom Index published by the Cato Institute. With the exception of Cuba (obviously) the Caribbean scores relatively well on both indexes, though these results should be read in conjunction with the other tables in the essay. Perhaps the one which stands out is Guyana, which score comparatively poorly in terms of freedom but also comparatively badly in terms of crime and education. Given the economic transformation currently underway in that country, how these indicators move in the coming year or so will tell us a lot.
